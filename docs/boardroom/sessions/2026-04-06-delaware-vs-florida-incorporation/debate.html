Round 1 — Yokum Taku [YES — DE + FL]

This is genuinely not a close call, and I want to walk through exactly why — because the Florida-only option looks cheaper and simpler in April 2026, and is going to look like a self-inflicted wound in 2027 or 2028 when you sit down with an acquirer or an institutional investor.

Let me start with where you actually are. RAV's stated exit strategy is acquisition by Marriott Vacations Worldwide, Hilton Grand Vacations, Wyndham, Expedia Group, or Booking Holdings. Every single one of those acquirers has an in-house M&A team and outside counsel that has done dozens of acquisitions. When that team opens the data room and sees the entity structure, they expect to see one of three things: a Delaware C-Corp, a Delaware LLC that will be converted to a C-Corp before close, or — very rarely — a Cayman or BVI parent for international reasons that don't apply to you. They do not expect to see a Florida LLC, and when they see one, three things happen.

First, the deal team flags it. Not as a deal-killer, but as friction. The diligence checklist gets longer. Counsel asks for the Florida operating agreement, the Florida tax compliance history, the Florida franchise tax filings, the local registered agent records. None of this is fatal, but every item adds time and legal fees on both sides.

Second, the conversion conversation starts. Almost any acquirer will require RAV to convert to a Delaware entity prior to or at close. I have personally watched founders absorb $40,000–$80,000 in legal fees during the closing weeks of an acquisition for exactly this — a conversion that should have happened on day one for $400 in filing fees.

Third, and this is the part founders almost never see coming: the conversion can create timing pressure on the deal itself. I have seen deals slip closing dates by two to four weeks waiting for Florida tax clearance. In a hot M&A process, two to four weeks is enough for an acquirer's strategic priorities to shift, for a competing bidder to drop out, or for the macro environment to move against you.

The Florida-only argument's strongest grounds: RAV's customers, properties, and operational center of gravity are in Florida. Florida has no state corporate income tax for LLCs taxed as partnerships. That argument is not crazy. It is just optimized for the wrong outcome — operational simplicity in year one — at the cost of strategic optionality in year three.

Here is the cost of the right structure. Delaware C-Corp formation: roughly $400 in filing fees, $300 annual franchise tax, and a Delaware registered agent at roughly $150 per year. Florida foreign entity registration: roughly $125 plus a Florida registered agent at roughly $150 per year. Total year-one cost: under $1,500. That is the entire incremental cost of preserving the optionality of every standard venture and acquisition path.

One more thing specific to your situation. Sujit and Sandhya can form a Delaware C-Corp today. Ajumon and Celin's OBA blockers do not prevent formation — they prevent those two co-founders from receiving founder stock until their employer disclosures clear. The standard solution: form the C-Corp now with the unblocked founders, reserve restricted stock for the blocked founders, and issue their stock the day their OBA clears. They must file 83(b) elections within 30 days of share issuance. That window is hard. I have seen founders pay seven-figure tax bills because they missed that 30-day window by a week.

If I were in the room: Engage a Wilson Sonsini-class startup attorney this week to file Delaware C-Corp formation documents, draft founder stock purchase agreements with four-year vesting and one-year cliff, and queue Florida foreign entity registration to follow within the same week.

My biggest concern with the other side: That the perceived simplicity of a Florida-only LLC will cost RAV three to six figures in legal fees and weeks of deal slippage at the worst possible moment.

Round 1 — Tonia Ouellette Klausner [YES — DE + FL]

I want to come at this from a different angle than Yokum, because Yokum is going to make the M&A case better than anyone, and Brian is going to make the Florida regulatory case better than anyone. The angle I bring is: when this platform gets sued — not if, when — what does the litigation look like, and how does the choice of corporate domicile affect the defense?

RAV is a consumer-facing platform that handles real money, real property access, and real travel. Your business model puts you at the intersection of three of the most litigated areas in consumer law: online marketplace platform liability, timeshare consumer protection, and financial transactions involving escrow. You will get sued. Most importantly for this question — you will be the named defendant in a putative class action filed by a plaintiff's attorney who specializes in TCPA, online marketplace, or consumer fraud cases.

Delaware corporate law has, for decades, developed a body of case law that is substantially more favorable to corporate defendants than Florida corporate law. That sounds technical but it has real consequences in three concrete areas.

First, internal affairs. If you have a falling-out among the founders — and you have four co-founders, two of whom are currently OBA-blocked, in a pre-revenue company, which is a statistically high-risk configuration for founder disputes — the law that governs whether a director can be removed, whether a shareholder can be diluted, whether a stock plan can be amended, is going to be the law of your state of incorporation. Delaware's body of fiduciary duty case law is the most developed in the country. Florida's is not.

Second, indemnification and exculpation. Delaware General Corporation Law Section 102(b)(7) allows you to put a provision in your charter that exculpates directors from monetary liability for breach of the duty of care. Section 145 lets you indemnify officers and directors broadly. Florida has analogous provisions but they are narrower in scope.

Third, forum and choice-of-law clauses. As a litigator who has defended marketplace platforms in class actions, I tell every client to put a forum selection clause in their terms of service. For Delaware-incorporated companies, those clauses are routine and reliably enforced. For Florida-only companies, you have a slightly weaker hand: a Florida court hearing a class action against a Florida company is going to be less receptive to arguments about why the case should be heard somewhere else.

Brian is going to push back on me, and he's going to push back correctly. None of what I just said displaces the substantive Florida regulatory work. If the Florida AG opens a consumer protection investigation under FDUTPA, your charter state is irrelevant. He is right about that.

What I am saying is: given that you have to do all the Florida-specific compliance work regardless, the marginal cost of also being a Delaware entity is small, and the marginal benefit on the litigation defense side is real and recurring.

If I were in the room: Draft the terms of service to specify Delaware law and Delaware forum (or AAA arbitration with Delaware as the seat), and align that drafting with the Delaware C-Corp formation.

My biggest concern with the other side: That a Florida-only structure creates a misalignment between the corporate domicile and the choice-of-law clauses in the terms of service, weakening the enforceability of the most important defensive provisions in a class action.

Round 1 — Patrick McKenzie [YES — DE + FL]

I'm going to focus on the payments and money-movement angle, because that's the one I see clearly and the one that gets understated in conversations like this.

RAV processes payments through Stripe Checkout, holds funds in PaySafe escrow, and disburses to owners through Stripe Connect. Every one of those rails has compliance machinery wrapped around it — Stripe's onboarding KYB, Stripe's underwriting, Stripe's risk team. I have spent enough time inside that machinery to tell you precisely what happens when you submit a Stripe Connect application as a Florida LLC versus as a Delaware C-Corp.

Submitting as a Delaware C-Corp: the application moves through standard underwriting. The entity type is what the engine expects. Approvals are typically fast.

Submitting as a Florida LLC: the application also moves through underwriting. It will probably get approved. But it lands in a slightly different bucket. For a marketplace with escrow mechanics and disbursements to third-party owners, the underwriting team's diligence questions are slightly more involved, because LLCs have more variation in their governance and ownership structures than C-Corps. None of this is fatal. It is friction. And the friction compounds when you also have an escrow product running through PaySafe.

The more important point: the underlying legal question for marketplace payments is who is the "merchant of record" for each transaction. If RAV is the merchant of record, then RAV owes consumer protection obligations under Reg E, chargeback obligations under card network rules, tax collection obligations under Wayfair, and — critically — potentially state money transmission licensing obligations in roughly 49 jurisdictions. State money transmission laws are some of the most fragmented and aggressive financial regulations in the United States.

Why does this matter for Delaware versus Florida? Because the analysis you have done about merchant-of-record status will be reviewed by Stripe's risk team, by PaySafe's compliance team, by your eventual D&O insurance carrier, and by every acquirer's diligence counsel. The standard structure that all of those reviewers expect to see, the one that minimizes their friction, is Delaware C-Corp. Not because Delaware C-Corp magically resolves any of the underlying questions — it doesn't — but because the entire payments compliance ecosystem has been built around Delaware C-Corps as the default.

One more thing about the Florida-only path. The Florida LLC is sometimes pitched as "simpler" because Florida has no state corporate income tax for partnerships. That is true on its face but misleading at scale. If RAV opens a beta to consumers in any state other than Florida — which it will, because your inventory and your traveler base are nationwide — the LLC's pass-through tax treatment becomes a multi-state tax compliance problem, because each member of the LLC owes taxes in each state where the LLC is doing business. C-Corps don't have this problem.

Last point. Yokum mentioned 83(b) elections and I want to underline it in red ink. The 83(b) election window is 30 days from share issuance. Not 30 business days. Thirty calendar days. Form Delaware now, issue stock cleanly, file the 83(b)s the next day, certified mail with return receipt, keep copies in three places.

If I were in the room: Run the Delaware C-Corp formation in parallel with a written merchant-of-record analysis of the current Stripe Connect + PaySafe escrow flow.

My biggest concern with the other side: That a Florida-only LLC creates structural friction with the entire payments compliance ecosystem for no offsetting benefit, and that this friction shows up at the worst possible moments — Stripe risk reviews, escrow audits, and acquirer diligence.
