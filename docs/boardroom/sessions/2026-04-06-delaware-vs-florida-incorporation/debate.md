# Boardroom Session — Delaware + Florida Foreign Entity vs. Florida-Only Incorporation

**Date:** 2026-04-06
**Board:** LEGAL-ADVISORY
**Question:** Should we "incorporate in Florida only" or "incorporate in Delaware and register in Florida as foreign entity?"

---

## Round 1 — Opening Positions

---

## Yokum Taku — Round 1

**Vote:** YES (Delaware + Florida foreign entity registration)

**Position:**

This is genuinely not a close call, and I want to walk through exactly why — because the Florida-only option looks cheaper and simpler in April 2026, and is going to look like a self-inflicted wound in 2027 or 2028 when you sit down with an acquirer or an institutional investor.

Let me start with where you actually are. RAV's stated exit strategy is acquisition by Marriott Vacations Worldwide, Hilton Grand Vacations, Wyndham, Expedia Group, or Booking Holdings. Every single one of those acquirers has an in-house M&A team and outside counsel that has done dozens of acquisitions. When that team opens the data room and sees the entity structure, they expect to see one of three things: a Delaware C-Corp, a Delaware LLC that will be converted to a C-Corp before close, or — very rarely — a Cayman or BVI parent for international reasons that don't apply to you. They do not expect to see a Florida LLC, and when they see one, three things happen.

First, the deal team flags it. Not as a deal-killer, but as friction. The diligence checklist gets longer. Counsel asks for the Florida operating agreement, the Florida tax compliance history, the Florida franchise tax filings, the local registered agent records, the Florida UCC searches. None of this is fatal, but every item adds time and legal fees on both sides — and acquirers absolutely notice when they're paying their lawyers to learn an unfamiliar state's corporate law to close a sub-$100M deal.

Second, the conversion conversation starts. Almost any acquirer will require RAV to convert to a Delaware entity prior to or at close. That conversion is not free. You will pay Florida and Delaware filing fees, you will pay tax counsel to confirm the conversion is tax-free under Section 368 or via a state-law conversion statute, and you will pay corporate counsel on both sides to draft and review the conversion documents. I have personally watched founders absorb $40,000–$80,000 in legal fees during the closing weeks of an acquisition for exactly this — a conversion that should have happened on day one for $400 in filing fees.

Third, and this is the part founders almost never see coming: the conversion can create timing pressure on the deal itself. Filing a state-law conversion is not instantaneous. You need board consent, member consent, articles of conversion filed in two states, certificates of good standing, tax clearance certificates (Florida has one and it is not fast). I have seen deals slip closing dates by two to four weeks waiting for Florida tax clearance. In a hot M&A process, two to four weeks is enough for an acquirer's strategic priorities to shift, for a competing bidder to drop out, or for the macro environment to move against you.

Now let me address the Florida-only argument on its strongest grounds. The argument is: RAV's customers, properties, resort relationships, and operational center of gravity are all in Florida. The Florida Vacation Plan and Timesharing Act applies regardless of where you incorporate. Your registered agent has to be local. Florida has no state corporate income tax for LLCs taxed as partnerships. Why pay Delaware franchise tax and Florida foreign entity fees when one filing covers it? That argument is not crazy. It is just optimized for the wrong outcome — operational simplicity in year one — at the cost of strategic optionality in year three.

Here is the cost of the right structure. Delaware C-Corp formation: roughly $400 in filing fees, $300 annual franchise tax (for a small company on the assumed-par-value method), and a Delaware registered agent at roughly $150 per year. Florida foreign entity registration: roughly $125 in filing fees plus a Florida registered agent at roughly $150 per year. Total year-one cost: under $1,500. Year-two-plus recurring: under $700. That is the entire incremental cost of preserving the optionality of every standard venture and acquisition path.

I want to flag one more thing specific to your situation. Sujit and Sandhya can form a Delaware C-Corp today. Ajumon and Celin's OBA blockers do not prevent formation — they prevent those two co-founders from receiving founder stock until their employer disclosures clear. The standard solution is well-known: form the C-Corp now with the unblocked founders as initial stockholders, reserve restricted stock or an option pool slice for the blocked founders, and issue their stock the day their OBA clears. Critically, they must file 83(b) elections within 30 days of share issuance. That window is hard. I have seen founders pay seven-figure tax bills because they missed that 30-day window by a week, and the IRS does not care.

If you form a Florida LLC today and convert to a Delaware C-Corp later, you have to do the IP assignment dance twice, the equity grant dance twice, and the cap table dance twice. Each dance is an opportunity to make an unrecoverable mistake.

**If I were in the room:** Engage a Wilson Sonsini-class startup attorney this week to file Delaware C-Corp formation documents, draft the founder stock purchase agreements with four-year vesting and one-year cliff, and queue Florida foreign entity registration to follow within the same week.

**My biggest concern with the other side:** That the perceived simplicity of a Florida-only LLC will cost RAV three to six figures in legal fees and weeks of deal slippage at the worst possible moment — when an acquirer is at the table and you have no leverage to push back on diligence demands.

## Brian T. Lower — Round 1

**Vote:** CONDITIONAL (lean Delaware + Florida foreign — but with non-negotiable Florida operational presence)

**Position:**

I've spent 26 years inside a Florida-headquartered vacation ownership company, and I've watched a lot of platforms try to enter this space from out-of-state corporate structures. So let me start where I'd push back on Yokum, and then tell you where I actually land.

The first thing I want to say is that the framing of this question — Delaware versus Florida — slightly misses what matters most for RAV. What matters most for RAV is not which state issues your charter. It is whether your operational and legal footprint inside Florida is robust enough to survive an inquiry from the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation, the Division of Florida Condominiums, Timeshares, and Mobile Homes, or — and this is the one that should keep you up at night — the Florida Attorney General's consumer protection division. Those regulators do not care whether your charter is in Dover or Tallahassee. They care whether you have a registered agent they can serve, an in-state address they can visit, and a documented compliance posture for the Florida Vacation Plan and Timesharing Act, Chapter 721 of the Florida Statutes.

So with that as context: yes, Yokum is right that Delaware C-Corp is the standard structure for the kind of acquisition path you're targeting. I'm not going to fight that. Marriott Vacations Worldwide, Hilton Grand Vacations, and Wyndham are all sophisticated acquirers and they expect Delaware entities. You will not be the first timeshare-adjacent company they look at, and you should not give their diligence team an unforced reason to ask uncomfortable questions on day one.

But — and this is the conditional in my vote — choosing Delaware does not get you out of any of the Florida-specific regulatory work. In some ways it makes that work more important, because once you're a foreign entity registered in Florida you've explicitly subjected yourself to Florida's jurisdiction for the purpose of doing business there, and you've handed the state's regulators a clean service-of-process pathway. So if you go the Delaware route, here is what is non-negotiable from where I sit:

One, you need a Florida-licensed registered agent at a real Florida address. Not a P.O. box. Not a virtual office in Miami you've never visited. A physical Florida address where someone can be served on a Tuesday afternoon and the papers will reach you the same day. The cheap online registered agent services are fine for this in year one, but understand what you're buying — you're buying a service-of-process address, not a compliance shield.

Two, before you accept a single Florida-resident transaction — and given that 117 of your resorts include Florida properties, this is essentially day one — you need a written legal opinion from a Florida-licensed timeshare attorney specifically addressing whether RAV's activities trigger registration as a "timeshare resale service provider" under Section 721.205 of the Florida Statutes. The definition is broad. The statute was written in an era before platforms like RAV existed, and it has been interpreted by Florida regulators in ways that have surprised platforms operating in adjacent spaces. I am not telling you the answer is yes — I genuinely don't know without reading your specific operational flow against the statute. I am telling you that you cannot operate without that opinion in your file.

Three, your owner onboarding warranty language has to require every owner to attest that their specific resort's CC&Rs and club rules permit rental through a third-party platform. Most resort governing documents have rental restriction clauses. Some explicitly prohibit secondary platforms. The TrustShield verification you have built handles deed and ID — it does not handle the resort-document permission question. And that is the question that gets you sued by a resort, not by a regulator.

Four — and this addresses the tension Yokum and I were always going to have — even if you incorporate in Delaware, the people who actually run RAV need to be reachable in Florida. Sujit is in Florida. That is good. Your check-in dispute escalation procedures need to go through someone who can answer a Florida resort's phone call during Florida business hours and speak to Florida law. That is not a charter question. That is an operational question. But it informs the charter question because — honestly — if you weren't planning to keep operations in Florida, I would tell you to incorporate wherever your operations actually are and fix the Florida regulatory exposure with foreign-entity registration when you cross that bridge. You're already over that bridge. The Florida exposure is real today.

So my conditional vote is: Delaware C-Corp is fine, register as a Florida foreign entity, but understand that you have not bought yourself anything by choosing Delaware except acquisition-readiness optionality. Every single piece of Florida-specific compliance work is exactly the same whether your charter is Delaware or Florida, and that work is the work that will actually determine whether this platform survives its first regulatory inquiry.

One last thing. I would not call Florida-only "wrong." I would call it "fine for a company that is never going to be acquired by a public hospitality company." If your honest exit case is a strategic acquisition by Marriott or Wyndham, listen to Yokum. If your honest exit case is a smaller transaction or an operator-led sale, the Florida-only path is genuinely defensible and saves you a small amount of money and complexity per year. The question to ask yourselves is which case you actually believe.

**If I were in the room:** Before either entity gets filed, retain a Florida-licensed timeshare attorney (Greenberg Traurig Orlando, Baker Donelson, or Stearns Weaver Miller all have the bench) to issue a written opinion on Section 721.205 applicability — that opinion's existence matters more than the charter state.

**My biggest concern with the other side:** That a Florida-only LLC creates a false sense of security — founders think they've handled "the Florida thing" by being a Florida entity, when in fact every Florida regulatory obligation applies regardless of charter state, and they've now also made themselves harder to acquire.

## Tonia Ouellette Klausner — Round 1

**Vote:** YES (Delaware + Florida foreign entity registration)

**Position:**

I want to come at this from a different angle than Yokum, because Yokum is going to make the M&A and venture financing case better than anyone, and Brian is going to make the Florida regulatory case better than anyone. The angle I bring is: when this platform gets sued — not if, when — what does the litigation look like, and how does the choice of corporate domicile affect the defense?

Let me explain why I say "when, not if." RAV is a consumer-facing platform that handles real money, real property access, and real travel. Your business model puts you at the intersection of three of the most litigated areas in consumer law: (1) online marketplace platform liability, (2) timeshare consumer protection, and (3) financial transactions involving escrow and refunds. You will get sued. You will get sued by an angry traveler who arrived at a resort and was turned away at the front desk. You will get sued by an owner who claims you took a booking against their wishes. You will get sued by a state attorney general's consumer protection division at some point in your first three years if you reach any kind of scale. And — most importantly for this question — you will be the named defendant in a putative class action filed by a plaintiff's attorney who specializes in TCPA, online marketplace, or consumer fraud cases.

Now, here is the part that matters for the Delaware versus Florida question. Delaware corporate law has, for decades, developed a body of case law that is substantially more favorable to corporate defendants than Florida corporate law. That sounds technical but it has real consequences in three concrete areas that I have personally litigated.

First, internal affairs. The internal affairs doctrine means that disputes involving the relationships among directors, officers, and shareholders are governed by the law of the state of incorporation. If you have a falling-out among the founders — and you have four co-founders, two of whom are currently OBA-blocked, in a pre-revenue company, which is a statistically high-risk configuration for founder disputes — the law that governs whether a director can be removed, whether a shareholder can be diluted, whether a stock plan can be amended, whether an officer can be terminated, and whether anyone has a fiduciary duty claim against anyone else is going to be the law of your state of incorporation. Delaware's body of fiduciary duty case law is the most developed in the country. It is predictable. Florida's is not. When I have advised founders on disputes, Delaware's predictability has been worth more than the entire incremental cost of forming there.

Second, indemnification and exculpation. Delaware General Corporation Law Section 102(b)(7) allows you to put a provision in your charter that exculpates directors from monetary liability for breach of the duty of care. Section 145 lets you indemnify officers and directors broadly. Florida has analogous provisions but they are narrower in scope and have less favorable case law. Once you start building a board — and you will, the moment you take outside money — your director candidates are going to ask about your D&O coverage and your charter exculpation language. Delaware-standard charter language is going to make that conversation easier and your eventual D&O premiums lower.

Third, and this is the one I care about most: forum and choice-of-law clauses. As a litigator who has defended marketplace platforms in class actions, I tell every client to put a forum selection clause in their terms of service requiring any disputes to be brought in a specific court, and a choice-of-law clause specifying the law that governs the terms. For Delaware-incorporated companies, those clauses are routine and reliably enforced. For Florida-only companies, you have a slightly weaker hand: a Florida court hearing a class action against a Florida company is going to be less receptive to arguments about why the case should be heard somewhere else under another state's law. That is not a fatal weakness — your terms of service can still specify Delaware law and forum even if you're a Florida entity — but it is one more place where the alignment of charter, terms, and litigation strategy matters.

There is a fourth thing I'll mention in passing: the way arbitration clauses with class action waivers are treated. Federal law under Concepcion and Epic Systems controls the enforceability of arbitration clauses, so this is mostly federal-question territory. But the procedural posture in which the question gets litigated — motion to compel arbitration, removal to federal court, the speed of federal versus state docket — can be subtly affected by where you're a citizen. Delaware citizenship is, frankly, useful in establishing diversity for federal jurisdiction in a way that Florida citizenship sometimes is not, because so many class plaintiffs are themselves Florida residents.

Now, Brian is going to push back on me here, and he's going to push back correctly. None of what I just said displaces the substantive Florida regulatory work. If the Florida AG opens a consumer protection investigation under FDUTPA — Florida's Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act — your charter state is irrelevant. Florida law applies, Florida courts hear it, Florida regulators control the timeline. He is right about that. And he is right that the Florida-specific compliance posture matters more than the charter choice.

What I am saying is: given that you have to do all the Florida-specific compliance work regardless, the marginal cost of also being a Delaware entity is small (under $1,500 in year one, by Yokum's reckoning, which matches my experience), and the marginal benefit on the litigation defense side is real and recurring. So I land in the same place as Yokum but for different reasons.

**If I were in the room:** Draft the terms of service to specify Delaware law and Delaware forum (or AAA arbitration with Delaware as the seat), and align that drafting with the Delaware C-Corp formation so that the choice-of-law clauses match the actual corporate domicile.

**My biggest concern with the other side:** That a Florida-only structure creates a misalignment between the corporate domicile and the choice-of-law and forum selection clauses in the terms of service, weakening the enforceability of the most important defensive provisions in a class action.

**Position:**

I represent the established timeshare industry. My job at ARDA is to make sure that new entrants into the secondary rental space don't create regulatory or reputational problems for the developers and operators who are paying ARDA dues and have spent decades building consumer trust in vacation ownership. Brian named the same five and that is not a coincidence — those five states cover roughly 70% of the inventory exposure for any meaningful timeshare resale or rental platform, because those are where the vacation ownership inventory is concentrated and where the state legislatures have written the most aggressive consumer protection statutes for this product category. Each of those five states has its own statutory definition of what counts as a "timeshare resale service," what counts as a "rental advertising service," and what triggers registration, bonding, escrow, and disclosure obligations. Those statutes apply to RAV regardless of whether your charter is in Delaware, Florida, Tallahassee, Honolulu, or Reykjavík. Your charter state does not get you out of any of them.

So when I look at the choice between "incorporate in Florida only" and "incorporate in Delaware and register in Florida as foreign entity," my honest reaction is: those options are nearly equivalent on the dimensions that ARDA cares about, and they both leave undone the work that I would want to see done before this platform launches. Both options put RAV inside Florida's jurisdiction. Both options require the same Section 721.205 analysis Brian flagged. Neither option does anything to address Hawaii, Nevada, California, or South Carolina exposure.

That said — since I have to vote, let me vote.

I lean toward Delaware plus Florida foreign entity registration, conditionally, for one reason that the rest of the room hasn't named yet: industry credibility. When RAV starts having conversations with brand protection counsel at Marriott Vacations, Hilton Grand Vacations, Wyndham, and Disney Vacation Club — and you will, because those brands' resort properties are in your ResortIQ database — the first slide in their internal memo about your platform is going to include your corporate structure. A Delaware C-Corp signals "this is a company built to be acquired by someone like us." A Florida LLC signals "this is a regional operator." Both are legitimate business models. But the conversations you have with those brand protection teams are different conversations depending on which signal they read first. Delaware C-Corp puts you in the conversation as a peer entity. Florida LLC puts you in the conversation as a counterparty to be regulated, contained, or shut down.

That signaling effect is small but real, and it compounds. ARDA membership, ARDA conference presence, and a Delaware C-Corp structure are three independent signals that, together, make resort brand protection teams treat a new entrant as part of the ecosystem rather than an unauthorized actor in it. None of those three things are individually decisive. Together they are the difference between a cease-and-desist letter and a phone call.

Now let me name the conditions on my conditional vote, because they are not optional and they matter more than the charter decision.

Condition one: before the LLC formation paperwork is filed in any state, RAV needs a multistate timeshare regulatory opinion covering at least Florida, Hawaii, Nevada, California, and South Carolina. Each opinion should specifically address (a) whether RAV's activities trigger registration as a timeshare resale service provider or rental advertising service in that state, (b) what disclosure obligations apply to listings of properties located in that state, and (c) whether the escrow arrangement triggers state-level money transmission or trust account licensing. That opinion is not optional. Brian flagged Florida — I am extending the same point to the other four states.

Condition two: the FTC's guidance on online consumer reviews, endorsement disclosures, and pricing claims applies to RAV's marketing, voice agent (RAVIO), and tool suite (the RAV Smart Suite). Before any of those go in front of public users, the marketing copy and the AI-generated claims need an FTC compliance review. This is not a charter question, but it is happening at the same time as the charter decision and I would rather raise it now than after a state AG sends a CID.

Condition three: ARDA membership. Apply this calendar quarter. The cost is modest and the relationship is the relationship. Whatever the charter state, the ARDA presence is the first credible signal to the established industry that RAV is a participant rather than a parasite.

So: conditionally yes on Delaware plus Florida foreign, with the understanding that the charter decision is the easy part of this conversation and the regulatory mapping work is the hard part that I have not yet seen evidence of in the materials available to me.

**If I were in the room:** Commission a five-state timeshare regulatory opinion (FL, HI, NV, CA, SC) from a firm with a real timeshare practice — Greenberg Traurig, Baker Hostetler, or Foley & Lardner — before filing entity paperwork.

**My biggest concern with the other side:** That the Florida-only path will be chosen because it feels operationally simpler, while the founders simultaneously underweight the four other states where their inventory creates real regulatory exposure that no charter choice will resolve.

## Patrick McKenzie — Round 1

**Vote:** YES (Delaware + Florida foreign entity registration)

**Position:**

I'm going to try not to repeat what Yokum, Brian, Tonia, and Jason have already said well, because the legal arguments for Delaware are strong and they've covered them. I want to focus on the payments and money-movement angle, because that's the one I see clearly and the one that gets understated in conversations like this.

Here's the reality of where you sit. RAV processes payments through Stripe Checkout, holds funds in PaySafe escrow, and disburses to owners through Stripe Connect. Every one of those rails has compliance machinery wrapped around it — Stripe's onboarding KYB (Know Your Business), Stripe's underwriting, Stripe's risk team, and Stripe's legal team. I have spent enough time inside that machinery to tell you precisely what happens when you submit a Stripe Connect application as a Florida LLC versus as a Delaware C-Corp, because I have seen the pipeline from both sides.

Submitting as a Delaware C-Corp: the application moves through standard underwriting. The entity type is what the underwriting engine expects. Beneficial ownership disclosures map cleanly to the standard form. The risk score is calibrated against a population of similar entities. Approvals are typically fast and the platform gets onboarded with minimal back-and-forth.

Submitting as a Florida LLC: the application also moves through underwriting. It will probably get approved. But it lands in a slightly different bucket. LLC entity types are common enough that the engine handles them, but for a marketplace with escrow mechanics and disbursements to third-party owners, the underwriting team's diligence questions are slightly more involved, because LLCs have more variation in their governance and ownership structures than C-Corps. You will be asked for the operating agreement. You will be asked about the relationship between the LLC's members and the platform's beneficial owners. None of this is fatal. It is friction. And the friction compounds when you also have an escrow product running through PaySafe, because PaySafe's compliance team is going to do their own version of the same review.

Here is the more important point. The underlying legal question for marketplace payments is who is the "merchant of record" for each transaction. If RAV is the merchant of record, then RAV is the entity that owes the consumer protection obligations under Reg E, the chargeback obligations under the card network rules, the tax collection obligations under Wayfair, and — critically — potentially the state money transmission licensing obligations in roughly 49 jurisdictions. If the owner is the merchant of record and RAV is operating as a payment facilitator routing funds between the cardholder and the owner via Stripe Connect's destination charges or separate charges and transfers model, then RAV's exposure shifts but does not disappear. Jason raised the money transmission licensing question and he was right to. State money transmission laws are some of the most fragmented and aggressive financial regulations in the United States, and the analysis of whether your specific Stripe Connect flow triggers licensing in a given state is fact-specific, structure-specific, and changes when the entity changes.

Why does this matter for Delaware versus Florida? Because the analysis you have done — or have not yet done — about merchant-of-record status, money transmission licensing, and state-by-state compliance is going to be reviewed by Stripe's risk team, by PaySafe's compliance team, by your eventual D&O insurance carrier, and by every acquirer's diligence counsel. The standard structure that all of those reviewers expect to see, the one that minimizes their friction and maximizes the chance they accept your existing analysis without requiring you to redo it, is Delaware C-Corp. Not because Delaware C-Corp magically resolves any of the underlying questions — it doesn't — but because the entire payments compliance ecosystem has been built around Delaware C-Corps as the default, and deviation from the default forces every reviewer to spend cycles figuring out whether your structure changes the analysis. Cycles are friction. Friction kills startups in subtle ways.

There is one more thing I want to say specifically about the Florida-only path. The Florida LLC option is sometimes pitched as "simpler" because Florida has no state corporate income tax and no franchise tax for LLCs taxed as partnerships. That is true on its face but it is misleading at the scale RAV is operating. If RAV starts processing real money and at any point opens a beta to consumers in any state other than Florida — which it will, because your inventory and your traveler base are nationwide — the LLC's pass-through tax treatment becomes a multi-state tax compliance problem, because each member of the LLC owes taxes in each state where the LLC is doing business. C-Corps don't have this problem. C-Corp tax is paid at the entity level, in one filing, and the founders' personal tax exposure is dividend-and-capital-gain-only. The "simpler" structure is not actually simpler once you have multistate revenue.

Last point. Yokum mentioned 83(b) elections and I want to underline it in red ink. The 83(b) election window is 30 days from share issuance. Not 30 business days. Not "approximately a month." Thirty calendar days. Missed 83(b) elections are the single most expensive recoverable mistake I have seen first-time founders make at the formation stage, and they are unrecoverable in the sense that the IRS will not grant an extension under any circumstances. Form Delaware now, issue stock cleanly, file the 83(b)s the next day, certified mail with return receipt, and keep copies in three places.

**If I were in the room:** Run the Delaware C-Corp formation in parallel with a written merchant-of-record analysis of the current Stripe Connect + PaySafe escrow flow — both should be done by the same week so the entity structure and the payments structure are aligned in the operational record from day one.

**My biggest concern with the other side:** That a Florida-only LLC creates structural friction with the entire payments compliance ecosystem (Stripe, PaySafe, card networks, state money transmission regulators) for no offsetting benefit, and that this friction shows up at the worst possible moments — Stripe risk reviews, escrow audits, and acquirer diligence.

## Yokum Taku — Round 2

**Final Vote:** YES (Delaware + Florida foreign entity)

**Who I'm arguing with most:** Brian Lower — specifically his framing that Delaware C-Corp "buys you nothing except acquisition-readiness optionality."
**Did I change my mind?** NO — but Brian, Jason, and Patrick collectively sharpened what I would have said about parallel tracks of work.

**Rebuttal:**

Brian, I want to engage your strongest argument directly. You said Delaware C-Corp "buys you nothing except acquisition-readiness optionality" and that every piece of Florida-specific compliance work is the same regardless of charter state. The first half is wrong and the second half is right, and the distinction matters.

You're right that the Florida regulatory work is the same in either path. The Section 721.205 analysis, the resort governing-document warranty language, the registered agent, the FDUTPA exposure — all of that applies regardless of charter. I accept that completely and I don't think anyone in this room would disagree.

Where you're wrong is in calling acquisition-readiness optionality "nothing." For a company whose stated exit strategy is acquisition by a public hospitality company, acquisition-readiness optionality is the entire game. RAV is not building a Florida lifestyle business. RAV is building a company that the founders intend to sell to Marriott Vacations, Hilton Grand Vacations, Wyndham, Expedia, or Booking Holdings. Every dollar spent on the platform, every line of code, every resort relationship, every test case, is implicitly paid for by the eventual acquisition multiple. The structural choices that preserve or impair that acquisition path are not "nothing." They are the most important non-product decisions a founding team makes.

You also raised the possibility that "if your honest exit case is a smaller transaction or an operator-led sale, the Florida-only path is genuinely defensible." I want to push back on this, gently. Founders almost always overestimate their ability to predict their exit. The whole reason institutional investors and M&A counsel insist on Delaware C-Corps as the default is that the structure preserves all paths simultaneously — VC financing, strategic acquisition, secondary sale, even an IPO if the company gets there. A Florida LLC eliminates several of those paths or makes them substantially more expensive. The asymmetry is large: Delaware preserves optionality cheaply; Florida-only forecloses optionality cheaply. When the cost of preserving optionality is under $1,500 a year, you preserve it. Always.

Tonia made a point I want to amplify. Her observation that Delaware's case law on internal affairs, fiduciary duty, and indemnification is more developed than Florida's matches my entire experience advising founder teams through disputes. RAV has four co-founders, two of whom are currently OBA-blocked, and the company is pre-revenue. That is a high-risk configuration for founder disputes. I have seen otherwise-functional founding teams blow apart in year two over equity disagreements, and when they do, the law that governs their charter is the law that governs their fight. Delaware predictability is not abstract — it is a tangible reduction in the cost of resolving the dispute when it happens. And it does happen.

Patrick's point about the payments compliance ecosystem is the one I would have undersold. I think about Delaware C-Corp as the institutional capital default, but Patrick is right that the same default operates downstream of formation through every piece of payments infrastructure. Stripe's risk underwriting, PaySafe's compliance review, the eventual D&O carrier, the eventual auditor — they are all built around the Delaware C-Corp shape.
