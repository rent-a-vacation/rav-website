The thing I want to redirect attention toward: Your platform has 8 policy documents in docs/support/policies/ at status: draft. Until those documents become status: active, every owner you onboard and every traveler who creates an account is doing so under no enforceable terms — no enforceable arbitration clause, no enforceable class action waiver, no enforceable privacy policy. If you launch beta with the policies still at draft, every user who signs up during that window has signed up under no terms. You cannot retroactively bind them to arbitration after.

Atlas is a 2-week task. The 8-document lawyer review is a 4-8 week task. Run them in parallel, not sequentially.

Critical items to flag in the 8 existing drafts:

  • Booking ToS: Disclaim RAV is a party to rental? Binding arbitration with class waiver? Specify jurisdiction?

On TCPA compliance for SMS: When you turn off SMS_TEST_MODE, every recipient must have provided prior express written consent specifically for SMS, separately from agreeing to the ToS. One TCPA class action with 10,000 affected users is $5M-$50M in statutory damages.

If I were in the room: Engage a Florida-based marketplace attorney this week. Hand them the 8 policy drafts. Tell them: "Atlas filing is in progress; we'll have the entity in 2 weeks; review these as platform documents."

My biggest concern with the other side: The 80th-percentile risk for RAV in Year 1-2 is a consumer class action — privacy, TCPA, automatic renewal, or a misrepresented cancellation policy.

Patrick McKenzie — Round 1

Vote: YES — Stripe Atlas, for the most boring possible reason: it eliminates the highest-friction step in standing up RAV's payment infrastructure.

Disclosure: I'm at Stripe. I have a conflict of interest on this question.

The Stripe activation problem: When you sign up for a Stripe account independently, Stripe's underwriting team evaluates your business. For a timeshare rental marketplace — historically a high-chargeback category — that conversation is non-trivial. Manual review takes 2-10 business days, transaction limits are constrained during review, and a denied application creates painful workaround paths.

Atlas pre-clears all of this. The activation is essentially instant. The day you complete Atlas filing is the day you can run a real transaction.

On Stripe Connect specifically: Your platform uses destination charges. The marketplace operator (RAV) is the merchant of record on every transaction. You are the party Stripe holds responsible for chargebacks. Atlas's Cooley templates know how to handle this. I have personally seen Gust-formed companies have issues during Stripe Connect onboarding because Gust's business profile language doesn't pre-anticipate marketplace structures.

On Mercury: Fine bank for marketplace startups. Banking-as-a-service on top of Choice Financial Group / Evolve Bank & Trust. Matters in two scenarios: (1) raising $10M+ — investors want primary banking moved; (2) credit line larger than Mercury's $5M ceiling. Neither matters at your stage.

On the merchant of record question: Card issuer's chargeback adjudication will look at RAV's ToS to evaluate chargebacks. If your ToS does not clearly establish that the cardholder is purchasing a service from RAV (the platform) rather than directly from the property owner, you'll lose chargebacks you should win. The merchant-of-record framing in your ToS needs to be explicit, not implicit.

On state money transmission licensing: RAV's escrow mechanism (PaySafe holds funds until resort confirmation) is functionally a money transmission activity in some states' interpretations. The standard structure for marketplace platforms is to use an established money services business. PaySafe is the right answer if PaySafe is what I think it is — but the founder needs a written legal opinion confirming this.

If I were in the room: Get your Stripe Disputes process documented before you take a single real transaction. Atlas gets you the live Stripe account; it does not staff your dispute response operation. The first 50 chargebacks define your processor relationship for the next two years.

My biggest concern with the other side: Everyone is treating Atlas as a corporate-formation decision. It's actually a payments-infrastructure decision wearing a corporate-formation costume.
