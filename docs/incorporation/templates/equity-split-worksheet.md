# Equity Split — Discussion Worksheet for the Founder Meeting

> **Audience:** RAV co-founders. The goal of this worksheet is to make the equity-split conversation **structured rather than verbal** so the final number feels fair, justified, and is something everyone can live with for the next 4+ years.

> **Time required:** ~60-90 minutes total. ~20 minutes filling in worksheets individually + ~40-60 minutes group discussion.

---

## Why we don't just default to 25/25/25/25

A flat equal split feels fair on the surface. It's "we're all equal partners." But "fair" and "equal" aren't the same thing — fair means the split reflects what each person is actually contributing.

For RAV specifically, there are some real asymmetries that an equal split would ignore:

1. **Sujit built the platform pre-formation.** ~12 months of work. 1,394 automated tests. Full Stripe + escrow + voice + chat integrations. 117 resorts indexed. Sustained effort that the other three didn't contribute to. An equal split means Sujit gives 75% of his completed work product to three people who didn't build it.

2. **Sandhya is OBA-cleared and full-time available.** Ajumon and Celin are still at day jobs and can't formally take their roles or stock until their OBAs clear. That's a real difference in commitment level today (though not necessarily forever).

3. **Risk asymmetry.** If the company fails in month 6, who's worst off? The full-time founders are giving up income. The day-job founders are not. That asymmetry deserves recognition.

4. **Future contribution.** Each of us is going to contribute different amounts going forward. Sujit will continue to be the technical lead. The COO/CPO/CFO roles are less about hours and more about domain ownership.

A flat 25/25/25/25 ignores all of this. It might still be the right answer — but it should be the right answer because we *decided* it was right after considering the asymmetries, not because it was the default.

## The process (run this in tomorrow's meeting)

### Step 1 — Each founder fills in this worksheet INDIVIDUALLY (20 min, no discussion)

Print four copies of this worksheet, or fork four Google Doc copies. Each founder fills it in **without consulting the others**. Use a pen — this isn't precise, it's a forcing function for thinking.

### Step 2 — Reveal simultaneously (5 min)

All four worksheets get put on the table at the same time. Compare the bottom-line splits each person came up with.

**Three possible outcomes:**

- **Tight consensus** (everyone's split is within ±3 percentage points per founder): Done. Take the median for each person, normalize to 100%, write it into the term sheet.
- **Moderate variance** (splits differ by 5-10 points): Discuss the factors people weighted differently. Most disagreements live in 1-2 specific factors (usually "value of past contribution" and "expected future hours"). Re-run the worksheet with shared assumptions on those factors.
- **Wide variance** (splits differ by >10 points per founder): Stop. Don't try to negotiate to a number tonight. The variance means the four of us have different fundamental views about the business and our roles. Schedule a follow-up meeting; consider a third-party (e.g., a startup advisor or the lawyer once engaged) to facilitate.

### Step 3 — Discuss + lock in (40 min)

Walk through:
- Where everyone's worksheets agree
- Where they disagree, and why
- What number we can all sign

**Hard rule:** the conversation ends with a written number for each founder, totaling 100%, that goes into the term sheet. No "we'll figure it out later." That's how this gets stuck.

Based on the asymmetries described above, a reasonable ROUGH starting point for discussion might look like:

|---|---|---|
| Sujit | 35-45% | Pre-formation platform build + full-time CTO role + idea origination |

| Ajumon | 17-22% | Part-time pending OBA, COO role |
| Celin | 15-20% | Part-time pending OBA, CFO role |

**This is a reference, not a recommendation.** Your worksheet might output very different numbers. The point of the worksheet is to derive a number from explicit factors, not to anchor on what someone else thinks.

If your worksheet outputs roughly the same range as above, that's confirming. If it outputs something very different, dig into why — it usually means the four of you are weighting one of the seven factors very differently.

## What to AVOID in this conversation

- **"Let's just split it equally to keep things simple."** — The simplest answer isn't always the right answer. Equal-split co-founder agreements are a leading cause of co-founder disputes 18 months later. The Y Combinator team has written about this extensively (Paul Graham: "[Don't worry about being fair — worry about being honest about each other's contribution]").

- **"Whatever works for everyone is fine with me."** — This sounds magnanimous but actually puts the equity decision on whoever speaks last. Each founder needs to put a number on the table, even if it's the same as someone else's.

- **"We'll figure out the split later when there's revenue."** — Atlas issues stock at incorporation. There is no "later." If we don't decide today, we're either delaying incorporation (bad — see Yokum's boardroom note) or making the decision under pressure inside the Atlas wizard.

- **Avoiding the past-contribution conversation.** Sujit has done a lot of pre-formation work. Pretending otherwise to "be fair" is itself unfair. Acknowledge it explicitly. Sujit might still choose to weight it lightly — that's his prerogative — but the discussion shouldn't avoid the topic.

- **Negotiating to a "round" number.** If the worksheet produces 37/22/21/20, don't round to "let's call it 35/25/22/18 because the numbers look nicer." The math from the worksheet IS the right answer; rounding away from it just introduces noise.

---

## What to AIM FOR

The conversation succeeds if:

1. We end the meeting with **four numbers totaling 100%** that all four founders agree to in writing
2. **Each founder can articulate the reasoning** for why their number is what it is — not just "we agreed to it" but "I get X% because of past contribution, future commitment, and role criticality"
3. **No one leaves feeling like they got a worse deal than they should have.** If someone's quietly resentful, that resentment compounds for years.
4. **We sign the term sheet within ~7 days** of this conversation, so we can start Atlas filing the same week.

The conversation fails if:

1. We end without a number, or with a "we'll decide next week" kicked-can
2. Anyone signs the number while privately disagreeing with it
3. The number is decided by who pushed hardest in the room rather than by structured reasoning

## Alternative methodologies (if the worksheet doesn't work for us)

**Foundrs.com / Co-Founder Equity Calculator** — a free online tool that walks through ~20 questions and outputs a recommended split. Often used in YC programs. Worth running independently as a sanity-check on whatever the worksheet produces.

**Slicing Pie (Mike Moyer)** — dynamic equity model where each founder's share is recalculated based on actual contributions over time. Powerful but operationally expensive to maintain. Not recommended for a 4-founder pre-launch company that wants a clean cap table for the eventual acquirer. Worth knowing about.

**Independent valuation by an outside advisor** — a startup advisor or the lawyer (once engaged) facilitates the conversation. This is the right call if the four of us can't get to consensus through the worksheet. It costs ~$500-2K depending on facilitator but breaks deadlocks.
