# Personal-Network Letter — Asking for a Timeshare Attorney Referral

> Reusable letter to send to friends, family, professional contacts, or anyone in your network who might know a Florida timeshare attorney (or be one step removed from one). The tone is conversational; the goal is to give the recipient enough information that they can make a meaningful introduction without needing a follow-up call.

> **How to use:**
> 1. Copy this file to a personalized version (e.g., `letters-sent/2026-04-26-to-[FRIEND_NAME].md`) — keep this template clean.
> 2. Replace `[FRIEND'S NAME]` and other `[BRACKETED]` placeholders.
> 3. Pick the closing tone (Casual / Professional) per your relationship with the recipient.
> 4. Send via whatever channel the relationship lives on (email / WhatsApp / text).

> **Why a written letter rather than a phone call:** Written gives the recipient something they can forward — and forwarding is what makes referrals actually happen. A phone call requires them to remember and re-summarize; a forwardable letter does the work for them.

---

## The Letter

> Subject (if email): Quick favor — looking for a timeshare attorney in Florida

---

Hi [FRIEND'S NAME],

I hope you're doing well. I wanted to update you on what I've been working on and ask a small favor — no obligation either way, just thought you might know someone.

### What I'm building

For about the last [X months / year], I've been building **Rent-A-Vacation** (https://rent-a-vacation.com) — a marketplace platform that connects timeshare property owners with travelers who want to rent unused vacation weeks. Think of it as the eBay or Airbnb for the timeshare rental market specifically.

Here's the problem I'm solving: the average timeshare owner pays around $1,500/year in maintenance fees whether they use their week or not. Industry data says vacation club properties run at 80% occupancy — meaning 20% of high-quality resort nights every year just go unused, while travelers who would happily rent at below-retail prices have no clean marketplace to do it through. Existing options (RedWeek, VRBO, Facebook groups) are fragmented and unverified. RAV is a real marketplace with verification, escrow, and a transparent bidding mechanism.

We're a small team of four co-founders (all based in Florida — three in [CITY/AREA, e.g., Jacksonville]) and the platform is essentially built. About 1,400 automated tests passing, full payment integration with Stripe, escrow, voice AI for help, the whole thing. We're now in the home stretch before opening to real users.

### Why I'm reaching out

We're about to incorporate as a Delaware C-Corp (the standard structure for tech companies) with foreign-entity registration in Florida (where we operate). Before we onboard a single owner, we need to engage a Florida attorney with **specific experience in timeshare law** to do a few connected things:

1. Confirm in writing that what we're doing — running a software marketplace for timeshare rentals — doesn't require us to register as a "timeshare resale service provider" under Florida's specific timeshare statutes. (We don't think it does — we're a rental marketplace, not a resale broker — but we need this in writing before we launch.)
2. Review the platform's terms of service, privacy policy, cancellation policy, and similar documents (we have eight policy documents drafted in plain English and ready for legal redline).
3. Confirm that our money-handling structure (we use an escrow service to hold booking funds until the resort confirms the reservation) doesn't create any state money-transmitter licensing issues.

The challenge: this is a narrow specialty. Florida has hundreds of business attorneys, but the ones who actually know timeshare law from the inside — who have represented Hilton Grand Vacations or Marriott Vacations Worldwide or Disney Vacation Club, or who have done work for ARDA (the industry trade association) — number maybe a few dozen. The big firms with that bench include Greenberg Traurig (Orlando hospitality practice), Holland & Knight (vacation ownership group), and Foley & Lardner. But I'd rather get to one of them through a warm introduction than send a cold inbound.

### The ask

If you happen to know:

- **Anyone who is a timeshare attorney in Florida**, or
- **Anyone who works (or has worked) at a major timeshare company** (Marriott Vacations Worldwide, Hilton Grand Vacations, Wyndham, Diamond Resorts, Disney Vacation Club) who would know who their company's outside counsel is, or
- **Anyone in the hospitality/vacation industry in Florida** generally who might be one degree removed from that bench

— I'd be grateful for an introduction. A simple "hey [name], meet Sujit — he's building something in your space and looking for legal counsel; here's his note" forward would be perfect. I have a more detailed write-up of what we're looking for ready to share if anyone wants more context before responding.

If nothing comes to mind right now, no problem at all — I appreciate you reading this far. And if you ever want a walkthrough of the platform, happy to set that up; we're proud of what we've built.

Thanks for thinking of me. Hope to catch up soon either way.

Best,
Sujit

**Professional:**

## What this letter is NOT

- **Not** a pitch for them to invest. We're not raising; this is an information request. Don't accidentally make it sound like a fundraising note.
- **Not** a request for free legal advice. Friends who are lawyers may want to help directly — that's lovely, but for this engagement we specifically need a Florida-licensed timeshare specialist, not a friend doing pro bono general counsel.
- **Not** a confidentiality risk. Everything in this letter is the same information that's on our public website + the same framing we'll use in the cold lawyer outreach. No NDA needed for sharing.
