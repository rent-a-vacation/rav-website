SECTION I: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Rent-A-Vacation operates an online marketplace that facilitates the rental of timeshare
periods by connecting timeshare owners ("Hosts") with prospective renters ("Guests").
The platform does not engage in the purchase, sale, transfer, or resale of timeshare
interests. This distinction -- between timeshare rental facilitation and timeshare
sales brokerage -- is the central legal question that determines the platform's
regulatory obligations in each U.S. jurisdiction.
This memorandum synthesizes legal research across all 50 states and the District of
Columbia, addressing the following core questions:

   Whether the platform's activities constitute real estate brokerage, travel agency,
   or marketplace facilitation under applicable state law.
   Whether state-specific real estate or travel agent licenses are required.
   What transient occupancy, hotel, and sales tax obligations apply to timeshare
   rentals.
   Whether the platform may hold funds, manage escrow, or process payments.
   What consumer protection disclosures and disclaimers are legally required.
   The platform's liability exposure in the event of Host failure or fraud.
Summary Finding: In the majority of U.S. jurisdictions, a platform that operates as a
neutral marketplace -- listing timeshare rentals without acting as agent, broker, or
fiduciary for either party -- does not require a real estate broker's license for the rental
facilitation function alone. However, Florida, California, Hawaii, Nevada, and
Arizona impose heightened requirements that demand immediate legal attention. Tax
remittance obligations are near-universal and represent the most operationally
significant compliance requirement.

SECTION II: BUSINESS MODEL CLASSIFICATION &
LEGAL POSTURE

2.1 The Marketplace Facilitator Model

Rent-A-Vacation's operational model is most accurately characterized as a
marketplace facilitator: a technology platform that provides listing infrastructure,
search functionality, payment processing, and communication tools, but does not
itself enter into rental agreements, hold title to any property, or act as agent for either
party.
This classification is legally significant because:
1. Real estate broker licensing statutes in most states apply to persons who, for
 compensation, act as agents in negotiating or completing real estate
 transactions. A platform that merely lists properties and facilitates direct
 contracting between owners and renters typically falls outside this definition.

2. Travel agency licensing requirements, where they exist, generally apply to
 entities that sell travel services on behalf of suppliers. A platform that connects
 owners directly with renters, without acting as agent for the owner, typically
 does not constitute a travel agency.

3. Marketplace facilitator tax laws, enacted in most states post-South Dakota v.
 Wayfair (2018), impose tax collection and remittance obligations on platforms
 that facilitate sales exceeding economic nexus thresholds -- regardless of
 whether the platform is the seller.

2.3 The "No Timeshare Sales" Designation

The platform's explicit exclusion of timeshare sales is legally protective. Florida Statute
§ 721.11 and California Business & Professions Code § 11003.5 define "timeshare
plan" broadly, but the rental of timeshare periods -- as distinct from the sale of
timeshare interests -- is generally treated as a transient occupancy matter rather than
a real estate transaction. This distinction must be clearly communicated in all platform
disclosures.

SECTION III: ATTORNEY ANALYSIS -- KEY LEGAL
QUESTIONS

Question 1: Is Rent-A-Vacation a Marketplace, Broker, or Travel Agent?

Short Answer: Marketplace facilitator, provided the platform does not negotiate
terms, act as agent, or hold itself out as representing either party.
Analysis: The platform's role as a neutral technology intermediary aligns with the
marketplace facilitator model recognized in most state statutes. The critical factors
are: (a) the platform does not negotiate rental terms on behalf of either party; (b)
rental agreements are entered into directly between Hosts and Guests; © the platform
does not hold itself out as a licensed real estate broker or travel agent; and (d) the
platform's compensation is a platform fee, not a commission on a real estate
transaction.
Recommendation: Ensure all platform documentation, Terms of Service, and
marketing materials consistently describe Rent-A-Vacation as a "marketplace" or
"listing platform." Avoid language suggesting agency, representation, or fiduciary duty
to either party. Have counsel review the Terms of Service and listing agreement
templates in Florida, California, and Hawaii specifically.
Risk Level: Moderate -- requires careful drafting and consistent messaging.

Question 2: Does Rent-A-Vacation Need State Licenses?

Short Answer: Generally no for rental facilitation alone, but Florida, California,
Hawaii, Nevada, and Arizona require heightened attention.
Analysis: Most states do not require a real estate broker's license for a platform that
facilitates timeshare rentals without acting as an agent. However, five states impose
specific requirements: Florida (§ 721.20 requires registration of "timeshare resale
brokers" but rental-only platforms may qualify for an exemption); California (DRE
oversight applies if the platform negotiates or solicits on behalf of owners); Hawaii
(Chapter 514E imposes strict requirements on "timeshare plan operators"); Nevada
(NRS 119A applies to timeshare marketing); Arizona (ARS § 32-2197 covers timeshare
sales agents but rental-only platforms may be exempt).
Recommendation: Obtain a formal legal opinion in FL, CA, HI, NV, and AZ on whether
the platform's specific activities trigger licensing requirements. If any Host is found to
be acting as an unlicensed broker, the platform could face secondary liability.
Implement a Host credentialing system that collects and displays license numbers for
commercial operators.
Risk Level: High in FL, CA, HI -- requires immediate legal opinion.

Question 3: Can Rent-A-Vacation Hold Funds?

Short Answer: Yes, as a payment processor/escrow agent, but with significant
restrictions in Florida, California, and Hawaii.
Analysis: The platform's payment processing function -- collecting rental payments
from Guests and disbursing to Hosts -- is permissible in most states under the
marketplace facilitator model. However, Florida (§ 721.20), California (Business &
Professions Code § 10145), and Hawaii (HRS § 514E) impose specific trust account
and escrow requirements on entities that hold consumer funds in connection with
timeshare transactions. These requirements may apply even to rental-only platforms if
the funds are held for any period before disbursement.
Recommendation: Implement a compliant payment escrow structure using a licensed
escrow agent or payment processor (e.g., Stripe Connect with separate trust
accounts). Ensure funds are disbursed to Hosts within the timeframes required by
applicable state law. Do not commingle Guest rental payments with platform
operating funds.
Risk Level: High in FL, CA, HI -- requires immediate structural compliance.
Question 4: Can Rent-A-Vacation Manage Escrow?

Short Answer: Not directly without an escrow license in most states; must use a
licensed third-party escrow agent.
Analysis: Escrow management -- holding funds on behalf of both parties pending
completion of a transaction -- requires an escrow license in most states. The platform
should not characterize its payment processing function as "escrow management."
Instead, it should use a licensed third-party payment processor or escrow agent to
hold and disburse funds, and describe this function as "payment processing" or
"payment facilitation."
Recommendation: Partner with a licensed escrow or payment processing provider.
Ensure the platform's Terms of Service clearly state that the platform is not an escrow
agent and that funds are held by a licensed third-party processor. Obtain legal
confirmation that the chosen payment structure complies with FL, CA, and HI
requirements.
Risk Level: Moderate -- manageable with proper payment infrastructure.

Question 5: What Disclaimers Are Required?

Short Answer: Multiple disclaimers required; varies by state and transaction type.
Analysis: Several categories of disclaimers are legally required or strongly advisable. A
marketplace disclaimer clarifying that Rent-A-Vacation is not a party to the rental
transaction. A no-timeshare-sales disclaimer. A non-broker/non-agent disclaimer. A tax
disclosure. A cancellation and refund policy. A limitation of liability clause. A Florida-
specific disclosure for Florida listings. A California-specific disclosure for California
listings. An escrow/fund-holding notice explaining how payments are processed and
protected.
Recommendation: Draft a comprehensive Disclaimer and Terms of Service document
reviewed by counsel in Florida, California, and New York. Display disclaimers
prominently on the homepage, listing pages, and checkout flow. See Section VIII for
complete disclaimer language.
Risk Level: Moderate -- manageable with proper legal drafting.
Question 6: What Is the Platform's Liability If an Owner Fails?

Short Answer: Limited if the platform clearly disclaims agency and uses proper
contractual protections, but consumer protection laws impose baseline obligations.
Analysis: If a Host fails to honor a rental agreement (e.g., cancels at the last minute,
provides inaccurate listing information, or fails to deliver the promised
accommodation), the platform's liability depends on: (a) whether the platform's Terms
of Service clearly disclaim agency and limit liability; (b) whether the platform collected
and disbursed funds in compliance with applicable law; and © whether the platform
made any representations about the quality or availability of the listed property.
Consumer protection laws in most states impose a baseline obligation of good faith
dealing that cannot be entirely disclaimed.
Recommendation: Implement a Host Guarantee or Guest Protection Policy that
provides a defined remedy (e.g., full refund within 5 business days) in the event of Host
cancellation. This policy both protects Guests and limits the platform's exposure to
consumer protection claims. Ensure the Terms of Service include a clear limitation of
liability clause capped at the platform fees paid.
Risk Level: Moderate -- manageable with proper contractual protections and a Guest
protection policy.

Question 7: What Is the Platform's Liability If Fraud Occurs?

Short Answer: Significant exposure under consumer protection and wire fraud
statutes; requires proactive fraud prevention measures.
Analysis: If a fraudulent listing results in financial harm to a Guest (e.g., a listing for a
property the Host does not own or cannot access), the platform faces potential liability
under: state consumer protection statutes (UDAP laws); the FTC Act § 5 (unfair or
deceptive acts); and potentially wire fraud statutes if the platform's payment
infrastructure was used to facilitate the fraud. The FTC's recent enforcement actions
(see Section VI) demonstrate active regulatory attention to timeshare-related fraud.
Recommendation: Implement a Host identity verification system (government ID
verification, resort ownership verification). Implement transaction monitoring for
patterns consistent with fraud. Establish a fraud response protocol including
immediate fund hold, investigation, and refund procedures. Obtain cyber liability and
fraud insurance. Ensure the Terms of Service include a fraud reporting mechanism and
cooperate fully with law enforcement investigations.
Risk Level: High -- requires proactive fraud prevention infrastructure.

SECTION VI: NOTABLE CASE LAW

The following cases are directly relevant to Rent-A-Vacation's legal posture and
operational risk profile.

Steines v. Westgate Palace, LLC

Court: U.S. Court of Appeals, 11th Circuit | Year: 2024 | Relevance: Military Lending Act
& Arbitration
The Military Lending Act (MLA) overrides the Federal Arbitration Act for timeshare
loans to active-duty servicemembers. Arbitration clauses in timeshare agreements
cannot be enforced against servicemembers covered by the MLA. Platform
Implication: If the platform's Terms of Service include an arbitration clause, it must
include an MLA carve-out for active-duty servicemembers. The platform should also
display the Military Lending Act Notice disclaimer on the payment flow for
servicemember accounts.

Palmer v. FantaSea Resorts

Court: New Jersey Appellate Division | Year: 2025 | Relevance: Deceptive Sales
Practices
The court awarded $1.64 million (trebled damages plus fee enhancement) when sales
representatives made verbal promises contradicting written contracts. The parol-
evidence rule does not shield bait-and-switch tactics. Platform Implication: All Host
listing representations must be accurate and verifiable. The platform should
implement a listing accuracy verification process and include a disclaimer that the
platform is not responsible for inaccurate Host-provided information -- while also
implementing a reporting mechanism for inaccurate listings.

Holden v. Holiday Inn Club Vacations Inc.

Court: U.S. Court of Appeals, 11th Circuit | Year: 2024 | Relevance: Credit Reporting &
Cancellation
Disputes involving complex contractual issues cannot be pursued under the Fair Credit
Reporting Act. The key test is whether alleged inaccuracies are objectively verifiable.
Platform Implication: If the platform reports payment disputes to credit bureaus or
uses credit data in Host/Guest vetting, it must ensure the disputed information is
objectively verifiable. Disputes involving subjective assessments of listing quality or
service should not be reported as credit events.

FTC & Wisconsin v. Carroll et al.

Court: Federal District Court | Year: 2026 | Relevance: Timeshare Exit Scheme
Enforcement
A $140 million judgment was entered against the operator of a timeshare exit scheme.
The scheme violated the FTC Cooling-Off Rule and used deceptive claims. The
defendant is permanently banned from marketing timeshare exit services. Platform
Implication: This case underscores the FTC's active enforcement posture in the
timeshare space. The platform must ensure it does not facilitate, advertise, or link to
timeshare exit services. All platform communications must be accurate and non-
deceptive. The "No Timeshare Sales" designation is legally protective in this context.

SECTION IX: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

Immediate Actions (0­30 Days)

  1. Obtain legal opinions in FL, CA, HI, NV, and AZ on whether the platform's
   specific activities trigger licensing requirements. Engage counsel in each state.

  2. Implement a compliant payment escrow structure using Stripe Connect or an
   equivalent licensed payment processor with separate trust accounts. Ensure
   funds are not commingled.

  3. Register as a marketplace facilitator for tax purposes in all states with
   applicable lodging/sales taxes. Implement an automated tax calculation and
   remittance engine.

  4. Deploy all eight required disclaimers on the platform. Ensure geo-targeted
   display of state-specific disclosures (FL, CA) on applicable listings.

  5. Implement Host identity verification using a licensed identity verification
   provider. Collect and display real estate/rental license numbers for commercial
   operators.

Short-Term Actions (30­90 Days)

  1. Draft and implement a Guest Protection Policy providing a defined remedy for
   Host cancellation or fraud. This both protects Guests and limits the platform's
   consumer protection exposure.
  2. Implement rental frequency monitoring to flag Hosts who exceed casual rental

   thresholds. Develop a tiered Host credentialing system (Casual / Multi-Property /
   Commercial).
  3. Have counsel review the Terms of Service and listing agreement templates in
   FL, CA, and HI. Ensure the MLA arbitration carve-out is included.
  4. Implement a listing accuracy verification process and a reporting mechanism
   for inaccurate listings. This reduces exposure under Palmer v. FantaSea Resorts.
  5. Obtain cyber liability and fraud insurance appropriate for a marketplace
   platform handling consumer payments.

Medium-Term Actions (90­180 Days)

  1. Engage a timeshare industry trade association (e.g., ARDA) to monitor
   legislative developments and participate in regulatory comment processes.

  2. Develop a state-by-state compliance calendar tracking tax remittance
   deadlines, license renewal dates, and legislative effective dates.

  3. Commission a formal legal audit of the platform's Terms of Service, privacy
   policy, and listing agreement templates against the requirements identified in
   this memorandum.
